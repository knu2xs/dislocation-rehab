Being able to actively lift your arm to about **160° at six weeks** is a very positive sign of recovery. It means your fracture and soft tissues are tolerating motion well, and you’re already meeting one of the protocol’s *eight‑week* goals. You still need to stay within the safe boundaries of your current phase, but you have more room to work on **control, endurance, and quality of movement** rather than chasing more range.

---

## What your current ability means
At this stage, the protocol allows:
- **Active‑assisted ROM** in all permitted planes  
- **Active scaption** up to 160°  
- **Active ER** at 0°–60° abduction  
- **Light functional use**  
- **No sling unless for comfort**

Your ability to reach 160° actively suggests:
- Your shoulder is not stiff.  
- You can begin refining movement patterns.  
- You can increase the volume of safe exercises without jumping ahead to strengthening.

These details matter more than the height of the lift.

### Light functional use
You can safely:
- Type  
- Use your arm for grooming  
- Perform light kitchen tasks  
- Drive an automatic car  

Avoid:
- Lifting anything heavier than a mug  
- Reaching quickly or overhead with load  
- Strength training or resistance bands  
- Behind‑the‑back stretching (IR stretching begins at 12 weeks)

## How to progress over the next 4 weeks while waiting for PT
You’re essentially following the protocol’s Week 6–12 phase:

- Increase **repetition volume**, not weight  
- Improve **smoothness** of motion  
- Work on **scapular control**  
- Gradually increase the amount of active motion you do upright  
- Keep ER within the allowed abduction range  
- Keep pain mild and short‑lived  

If you maintain this, you’ll be well‑prepared for the 12‑week strengthening phase once you reach it.

---

## When to pause or adjust
Stop or reduce intensity if you notice:
- Pain that spikes sharply  
- Pain that lasts more than 24 hours after exercise  
- New weakness or inability to lift the arm  
- Clicking or catching that wasn’t present before  

If any of these occur, contacting your clinician is important.
